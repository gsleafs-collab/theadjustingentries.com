Assurance

Review, audit, or compilation: which engagement do you actually need?

GM Gurjot Mann, CPA  ·  Jun 4, 2026  ·  7 min read
AE

When a lender or shareholder asks for "financial statements," they often have a specific level of assurance in mind, even if they do not use the word. The three options are not just price points. They differ in how much work the accountant does and how much comfort the reader can take from the result.

Compilation (Notice to Reader)

The accountant assembles financial information provided by management into statement format. No assurance is expressed. It is useful for internal purposes or for stakeholders who already know the business well. It is the lightest and least costly option, and it says so right on the page.

Review engagement

Here the accountant performs inquiry and analytical procedures to obtain limited assurance: nothing has come to their attention suggesting the statements are materially misstated. It is a middle ground, common when a bank wants more comfort than a compilation but does not require a full audit.

Audit

An audit provides reasonable assurance, the highest level offered. The auditor gathers evidence through testing, confirmation, and substantive procedures, and expresses an opinion on whether the statements are fairly presented. It is the most rigorous and the most expensive, and it is often required for larger entities, certain lenders, or regulatory reasons.

More assurance is not always better. It is about matching the level of comfort to who is relying on the numbers.

How to choose

  • Ask the user of the statements. The lender, investor, or regulator usually dictates the minimum.
  • Weigh cost against comfort. Do not pay for an audit when a review satisfies everyone who matters.
  • Think ahead. If you expect to raise capital or sell soon, building an audit history early can pay off.

If you are not sure which one a stakeholder actually needs, ask before you commission anything. The wrong level either wastes money or fails to satisfy the request, and both are avoidable with one conversation.
